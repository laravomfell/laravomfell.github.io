Research

My research lies at the intersection of the statistical and social sciences. Most contemporary research on policy design has been shaped by the available information: coarse and administrative data feeding into models that cannot hope to capture complex social processes. This is particularly worrisome for social phenomena that vary with time and space. My research is focused on developing and deploying the next wave of statistical methods to exploit the increasing availability of detailed individual-level data.

The relevance of individual differences for policy design

I am currently working on new models of police officer decision-making in the context of ethnic bias. Existing work often overlooks spatial information and individual-level heterogeneity. In contrast, my work seeks to bridge this by leveraging state of the art techniques in spatial statistics to address these questions. For example, I am investigating officer heterogeneity in the context of team structures using Bayesian hierarchical models.

Robust inference of count models (with Jeremias Koblauch)

Researchers trying to model count data frequently face model selection problems. Without detailed knowledge on the data-generating process it can be tricky to choose between zero-inflated, over-dispersed or standard count models and subtle features of the data heavily bias these decisions. In this project we build on research on robust divergences where parameter estimation is not as sensitive to model misspecification. Our goal is to demonstrate the usefulness of this approach to a wide range of count data problems, using both standard Bayesian and frequentist inference.

Household budgeting in the face of tight rental markets

In a developing line of work, my collaborators and I investigate how increases in rent impact household budgeting and how individuals deal with competitive rental markets.
